  2. What is the difference between "genotype" and "phenotype"?

    The genotype is the hereditary information passed on from one generation to the next while the phenotype is the expression of the genotype into observable characteristics.

  3. Define the following terms:
    • pleiotropy

      The expression of several different phenotypic effects from a single genotypic alteration.

    • allele

      A pair or series of genes at a particular "locus" on each of the chromosomes in a diploid pair.

    • genome

      The complete sequence of DNA base-pairs.

    • gene

      Information responsible for the eventual creation of one or more proteins.

    • Intron splicing

      Parts of the DNA which are not expressed as proteins are often rearranged by various means, aka spliced.

    • Positive Selection

      A gene in the germline that provides some benefit is preserved through positive selection.

    • Negative Selection

      A gene in the germline that has a neutral or deleterious effect is likely to lead to death (negative selection).

    • Polymerase

      The biomachinery that copies DNA.

    • Phylogenetic Relationships

      An attempt to place gene sequences in an evolutionary tree showing relationships between current and past mutations.

    • Transitions

      When a purene or pyramidene base swaps with its own kind (e.g. A with G, or C with T).

    • Transversions

      When a purine or pyramidene base swaps with the other kind (e.g. A with C, or A with T).

    • Homology

      Similarities between genetic sequences between different species, i.e. a search to uncover shared ancestry in the evolutionary tree.

    • Paralogy

      A duplicated gene that has found a home in some other part of the genome.

  4. How many possible unique human genomes are there?

    More than the estimated number of particles in the universe... 43,000,000,000

  5. What are the causes of genetic variation?

    So many! There are chemical factors that can replace base bairs; there are chromosomal swaps and mutations; sometimes there can be frame shifts while copying DNA... I've probably missed several as well.

  6. What are the error rates for copying RNA viruses and how does this compare to bacteria?

    RNA viruses have a much higher error rate, e.g. 1 in 105 as compared to about 1 in 1010 in bacteria.

  7. What happens to Drosophila when they have the Antennapedia mutation in the Hox gene?

    Legs for antennae!

  8. Why is gene duplication important to evolution?

    With a "backup set" of information, the duplicate can be altered with less chance of negative selection.

  9. What role does a knowledge of selection and evolution play in the treatment of cancer?

    There is a certain toleration for mutation built into the system so that positive change can occur; however, since a lot of mutation would be disastrous, the cell has evolved mechanisms that help it preserve the accuracy of the genome. If we can tap into these natural mechanisms then perhaps cancerous prolificacy of cells can be mitigated.

  14. What is the difference between Eukaryotic cells and Prokaryotic cells?

  15. What is the mitochondria and what is its function?

  16. What is the Golgi Apparatus and what is its function?
