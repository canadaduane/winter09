  1. How do yeast and fruit flies compare in terms of the percentage of genes with introns?

    Fruit flies have many more introns.

  2. How is an open reading frame (ORF) defined?

    It is the sequence of DNA, usually between start and stop codons, that are expressed as genes, i.e. proteins.

  3. Define the following terms:
    • PCR

      Polymerase Chain Reaction: the heating and cooling of DNA, in the presence of polymerase, to create mass replication.

    • BAC

      Bacterial Artificial Chromosomes: DNA portions "of interest" are inserted into bacterial chromosomes and then amplified by the bacteria. The DNA sequence can later be determined.

    • CpG island

      Cytosine-phosphor-Guanine island: hypomethylated DNA with "C-G" sequence(s). These regions are often involved in gene expression.

    • TATA Box

      Gene promoter region in Eukaryotes.

  7. Why was E Coli used for early genetic studies? It is easily grown and genetically easy to manipulate.
  8. What is an Okazaki Fragment?

    During DNA replication, polymerase copies the 5' to 3' strand in place, while the complementary strand (3' to 5') must be base-paired in reverse using small segments of DNA, which are the "Okazaki" fragments.

  9. What mechanisms exist to correct errors in your DNA copying mechanism?

    The polymerase checks for incompatible base-pairs and directs nucleases to slice up and then re-ligate the incorrect sequence.

Notes from pre-reading

  • 8 histones + DNA wrapped = nucleosome
  • HAT => histone acetyltransferase => "acetylation" = unwinding + opening of DNA for reading
  • HDAC => histone deacetylase => "deacetylation" = compaction + closing of DNA
  • DNA Polymerase III copies DNA from 5' to 3' direction
  • 5' -> 3' is called "downstream", and is the only direction the polymerase can assemble new strands in vivo
  • The 5' end is a phosphate group, the 3' end is a hydroxyl group
  • By convention, nucleotide sequences are recorded "downstream" as "left to right" in written text
