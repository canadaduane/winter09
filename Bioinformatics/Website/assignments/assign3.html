Assignment 3

  1. What is a Markov Process?

    A process that can be modeled without any history, i.e. the current state leads directly to the next state without knowledge of past states.

  2. Why do CpG islands appear in promoter regions?

    The sequence of several hundred (mostly) C-G nucleotides is uncommon as far as exon information goes, and it therefore can act as a flag for polymerase binding to occur. Because cytosine (C) can methylate over evolutionary time into thymine (T), only those "islands" with selective pressure will maintain long sequences of C-Gs.

  4. Is the DNA in each of your cells identical? Or are there some cells that manipulate their DNA?

    Life is amazing! "If it can happen, it will happen". As it turns out, some cells--cells belonging to the human immune system, for example--rearrange their own DNA to accomplish their goals. Some bacteria also regulate genes by transposing certain DNA segments.

  5. Name 3 ways of performing gene expression regulation.

    Activator proteins (bind to DNA sites and possibly bend the DNA in such a way that the DNA polymerase is better able to access the nearby promoter region), inhibitor proteins (i.e. that block promoter regions), and DNA rearrangement as mentioned above in the immune system.

  6. What is an operon?

    An operon is a multi-gene mRNA segment that is encoded as a group. Consequently, promoter regions affect gene expression equally among those genes in the operon.

  7. Explain the regulation of beta-galactosidase in E coli.

    Sure! It's quite neat. Basically, beta-gal is necessary for the breakdown and usage of energy from lactose. A small amount of lactose permease is usually hanging around, and allows a very small amount of lactose into the cell, if lactose is present. Lactose, it turns out, binds to a protein that normally blocks the promoter site near the gene that encodes beta-gal. When the lactose binds to that protein, it often breaks loose from the promoter region of the DNA, thus allowing DNA polymerase to begin coding mRNA for the beta-gal (as well as more lactose permease). Consequently, more lactose is allowed in to the cell, and the cell is able to use the lactose due to the prolification of beta-gal.
